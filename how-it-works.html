How the Life in the UK test works

A plain-English walk-through of what the test is, who needs it, how to book, what happens on the day, and what to do if you don't pass first time.

Who needs to take it

Most people applying for indefinite leave to remain (settlement) or British citizenship (naturalisation) must pass the Life in the UK test, if they are aged between 18 and 64. It forms part of the "Knowledge of Language and Life in the UK" requirement set by the Home Office.

You only ever need to pass it once. A pass never expires, so if you took it for your settlement application, you do not sit it again when you later apply for citizenship. Holding EU settled status does not remove the requirement for citizenship.

What's on the test

You answer 24 multiple-choice questions in 45 minutes, and you need 18 correct (75%) to pass. The questions are drawn from the official handbook, covering four broad areas:

  • The values and principles of the UK
  • UK history, from early Britain to modern times
  • Modern society, culture, sport and traditions
  • How government, the law and public institutions work

The history section catches most people out, because it leans heavily on specific dates, names and events. You can sit the test in English, Welsh or Scottish Gaelic — you choose the language when you book.

How much it costs

Each attempt costs £50, paid online at the time of booking. There are no discounts for retakes — every attempt is a fresh £50.

How to book

The test can only be booked through the official government service. Be wary of look-alike sites that add their own fees on top. A few things to know:

  • Book at least 3 days (72 hours) in advance — there is no walk-in option.
  • You'll need an email address, online payment, and an accepted form of ID (such as a passport, biometric residence permit, or an eVisa share code).
  • There are over 30 test centres across the UK, and you choose from the ones nearest your address.
  • A refund is only possible if you cancel at least 3 days before your appointment.

On the day

The test is taken in person on a computer at the centre — there is no remote or online version of the real test. To avoid a wasted fee:

  • Bring the same original ID you booked with. The name on your booking must match your ID exactly, including middle names.
  • Wrong, expired, or mismatched ID means you cannot sit the test and will not be refunded.
  • Your photo is taken at the centre on the day.
  • You can't bring children or other family members into the test.

Your result and the URN

You're told whether you passed verbally, on the day. If you pass, you receive a Unique Reference Number (URN) by email rather than a paper certificate. Keep it safe — you'll quote it in your settlement or citizenship application, and the pass behind it never expires.

If you don't pass

There's no limit on attempts. If you don't pass, you can retake the test as many times as you need — but you must wait at least 7 days before sitting again, and pay the £50 fee each time. The cheapest way through is preparation: practise under timed conditions until you're comfortably scoring well above 18 before you book.

Who's exempt

You may not need to take the test at all if any of these apply:

  • You are under 18, or 65 or over, at the date of your application.
  • You have already passed it previously — you simply reuse your URN.
  • You have a long-term physical or mental condition that makes it unreasonable to prepare for or take the test. This needs a medical waiver supported by evidence; it is not granted automatically.

A common misunderstanding: holding a degree taught in English does not exempt you from the Life in the UK test. That relates to the separate language requirement below.

The English language requirement is separate

The "Knowledge of Language and Life" requirement has two parts. The Life in the UK test covers your knowledge of UK life and institutions. You must separately meet an English language requirement (usually around B1 level), proven through different evidence such as an approved English qualification or a degree taught in English.
